## Introduction: Why Discuss "Letting Go" in Agriculture Now?
Does smart agriculture make farmers autonomous? Asking this question evokes a somewhat strange sensation. Sensors measure soil conditions, AI suggests optimal fertilization timing, and drones spray pesticides. With so much being "automated," farmers seem rather busier and more anxious than before. The machines have multiplied. However, has the farmer's ability to "read" their own farmland actually increased?

Much of the smart agriculture currently in widespread use designs farmers not as "decision-makers," but as "executors of instructions." The system provides the answers, and the farmer follows them. This appears efficient. But when the answer-providing system breaks down, or when an unforeseen situation occurs, what do farmers rely on to make judgments? This is not merely a question of technology theory. It is a fundamental question of what a farmer is.

### From "Controlling Complexity" to "Utilizing Complexity"
Modern agriculture has treated the complexity of farmland as a "problem to be eliminated." Weeds are eradicated with herbicides, pests with pesticides, and soil heterogeneity is masked by uniform fertilization—by erasing "noise," it has sought to transform farmland into a manageable production apparatus. In terms of short-term yields, this strategy has worked. Yet, the price paid was steep. The diversity of soil microbiomes was lost, natural predators vanished, pesticide-resistant pests emerged, and the capacity to adapt to climate change declined. A homogenized system is strong against expected changes, but fragile against unexpected ones.

Agroecology fundamentally shifts this paradigm. Depending on the species, weeds can improve soil and attract natural predators. Pest distribution patterns serve as indicators of soil and vegetation health. Heterogeneity within a field becomes the rationale for placing multiple crops in their optimal micro-environments. Complexity is not an enemy to be eliminated, but a resource to be utilized—this is the core assertion of agroecology.

However, a paradox exists here. For a farmer to actually make such ecological judgments, years of observation and trial-and-error are required. While agroecology points in the right direction, it has not sufficiently laid out a concrete path for farmers to begin walking in that direction.

### The Question of this Book: How to Use Technology to Let Go of Technology
The starting point of this book is a phrase born from the author's own five years of practical experience: "Using technology to let go of technology."

I set up a homemade agricultural sensing system in my field using a Raspberry Pi and a smartphone. It yielded data, and it offered suggestions. But as the years passed, I noticed something. The time I spent looking at the system had decreased. When I went to the field, I could understand more just by being there. The things the sensors used to tell me were now known by my own eyes, feet, and hands. The system hadn't "disappeared"; rather, it had dissolved into my agricultural practice.

This book argues that this is not a coincidence. Technology can be designed not to make farmers permanently dependent on systems, but as a "scaffold" for farmers to acquire autonomous judgment capabilities. A scaffold is removed once the building is complete. Similarly, technology should be "let go of" as a dictating machine once the farmer's ecological intuition is cultivated.

But the inquiry of this book does not end there. What does the autonomous farmer let go of next? I propose that by letting go of a *closed autonomy*—the monopolization of knowledge confined only to one's own land—and loosely connecting with the practices of other farmers, a larger ecosystem of knowledge might emerge. This is the concept of the "Double Letting Go" presented in this book.

### Structure of this Book
This book consists of three parts and a conclusion. Part 1 applies Vygotsky's educational psychology theory of "Scaffolding" to agriculture, discussing how farmers' ecological intuition is formed. Part 2 introduces the philosophy of technology (postphenomenology) and Actor-Network Theory (ANT) to ask where technology goes after the scaffold is removed. Part 3 confronts a difficult problem: how to share deeply acquired local knowledge with others—the "Dilemma of Locality"—and presents a vision for a Digital Agroecology Commons. The Conclusion synthesizes these theoretical consequences into a practical manifesto.

The propositions developed in this book are all "theoretical hypotheses awaiting empirical verification." If theory is detached from the concrete foundation of actual farming practices, it spins its wheels in the void. With this awareness, this book aims to provide "theory as a foundation for muddy practices."

## Part 1: Achieving Autonomy with AI as a "Scaffold" — Acquiring Ecological Intuition

### 1. The Tension Between Digital Technology and Agroecology
When digital technology entered agriculture, it was perhaps not to make farmers smarter, but to think on their behalf—while this may sound like an exaggeration, many widespread smart agricultural systems today lean in that direction. Sensors measure soil moisture, and AI notifies the farmer, "Please irrigate tomorrow morning." If you photograph a pest on your smartphone, the app responds, "Spray this pesticide at XX ml/10a." Information has become abundant. Judgments have diminished.

Agricultural engineers such as Klerkx et al. point out that much of current digital agriculture aims for the "automatic provision of optimal solutions." In light of the goal of efficiency, this is an appropriate design. But from the perspective of agroecology, this design holds a fundamental contradiction. The core of this contradiction lies in the view of knowledge. The knowledge that smart agriculture assumes is "universal and standardizable." However, the knowledge handled by agroecology is inherently "site-specific and context-dependent."

Agroecology perceives agricultural production as the application of ecological processes. The production capacity of a field is determined by its unique microbiome, soil structure, topography, and microclimate. Nitrogen fixation through the symbiosis of leguminous plants and rhizobia, or pest suppression by diverse vegetation attracting natural predators, cannot be replicated with nationwide uniform prescriptions. As agricultural researcher Duru et al. state, agroecological productivity is achieved not through the "control of complexity," but through the "utilization of complexity." If so, systems that deliver "answers" to farmers distance them from agroecology. What farmers need are not optimal solutions, but the ability to decipher the unique complexity of their own land—"ecological intuition."

### 2. Redesigning Technology as a "Cognitive Partner"
Here, insights from educational psychology cut sharply into the seemingly distant context of agriculture. The concept of the "Zone of Proximal Development (ZPD)" proposed by Vygotsky (1978) provides a crucial insight into the nature of learning. The ZPD is the area of tasks that a learner "cannot yet accomplish independently, but can achieve with appropriate assistance." "Scaffolding" is the process of providing that assistance and gradually withdrawing it as the learner's proficiency increases.

The concept of scaffolding, proposed by Wood, Bruner, and Ross (1976), has been consolidated into three main characteristics through subsequent cognitive science research. First is "Responsiveness"—dynamically adjusting the level of support according to the learner's current capabilities. Second is "Fading"—gradually reducing support as the learner's autonomy increases. Third is "Internalization"—the process by which knowledge and judgment capabilities previously provided externally take root internally within the learner. The most paradoxical core of this concept is that the ultimate goal of the scaffold is to render itself obsolete.

Cognitive scientist Roy Pea extended this to technology, arguing that appropriately designed technology can function as a "Cognitive Partner." Furthermore, Reiser presents two core mechanisms for scaffolding via technology. One is "Structuring"—breaking down complex tasks into manageable sub-tasks for the learner. The other is "Problematizing"—prompting conscious observation and reflection by questioning phenomena that learners tend to take for granted.

It is this latter concept, *Problematizing*, that holds special meaning in the context of agroecology. For a farmer, the scaffold must not be "Please irrigate this section today," but rather, "Why do you think the northern section dries slower than the southern section?" Posing a question instead of delivering an answer—this shift in design philosophy transforms technology from a "dictating machine" into a "cognitive partner."

### 3. How "Ecological Intuition" is Acquired
What does it mean for a farmer to "read" a field? Veteran farmers know the moisture status from the resistance of the soil when they step on a ridge; they sense nutrient excesses or deficiencies from subtle dullness in leaf color; they read the characteristics of the land from the proliferation of specific weeds. This is a "sense" derived from experience, but not entirely non-verbal, wild intuition. It is an embodied understanding formed through years of repeated observation and practice—what philosopher Michael Polanyi called "Tacit Knowledge." "We can know more than we can tell."

How is this ecological intuition as tacit knowledge formed? The model of skill acquisition described by cognitive scientists Stuart and Hubert Dreyfus is instructive. A novice starts with "reliance on explicit rules," an intermediate moves to "partial pattern recognition of the situation," and an expert reaches an "intuitive grasp of the whole situation"—at this stage, they instantly grasp the state of the whole field and make judgments without referring to a rulebook.

**Table 1: The Stage Model of Mastery and the Acquisition of Farmers' Ecological Intuition**

| Stage | Dreyfus's Description | Farmer's State (Example) | Role of the System |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1. Novice | Reliance on explicit rules | "I irrigate exactly as the AI notifies me." | Instruction / Structuring |
| 2. Advanced Beginner | Partial pattern recognition | "This section tends to dry out easily." | Questioning / Promoting awareness |
| 3. Competent | Contextual and goal-oriented judgment | "Let's wait and see this week." | Support recording / Promoting reflection |
| 4. Proficient | Intuitive grasp of the whole situation | "I know just by going to the field." | Recedes into the background |
| 5. Expert | Intuitive and embodied immediate response | "I understand the state the moment I see it." | Completely transparent |

*Note: Stages progress from Novice to Expert. Created by the author based on Dreyfus & Dreyfus (1986).*

When a farmer in the early stages of transition faces agroecological practices, their greatest cognitive barrier is the state of "not knowing what to observe." As cognitive psychologist John Sweller demonstrated, learning functions effectively only when cognitive load on working memory falls within an appropriate range. The first role the system plays as a scaffold is controlling this cognitive load. By focusing observation, such as "First, please pay attention to changes in soil moisture in the northern section," the farmer begins to slowly learn "how to read the complex system of the field."

However, merely observing does not generate tacit knowledge. As the SECI model by Nonaka and Takeuchi (1995) shows, tacit knowledge is formed exclusively through embodied practice and the direct experience of its outcomes. The system asks, "Why do you think the drainage in this section is good?" The farmer observes in their own way and tries leaving specific weeds. As a result, the soil moisture changes. Through the repetition of linguistically connecting this experience with ecological processes, the farmer eventually reaches a state of "knowing without asking"—ecological intuition.

Crucially, the system's questioning does not "directly install" intuition. The subject forming the intuition is always the farmer's own chain of embodied practices and experiences. Technology is merely a catalyst that activates, directs, and continuously supports that learning loop. This distinction is a fundamental point directly tied to the book's theme of "letting go of technology."

**Table 2: The Formation Process of Ecological Intuition through Cognitive Scaffolding**

| Phase | Learning Process | Role of AI System (Cognitive Scaffold) | Farmer's Practice and Cognition |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Problematizing | Presenting questions based on anomalies or subtle differences | Conscious focalization of observation targets |
| 2 | Structuring | Structuring data for hypothesis testing | Embodied intervention (fine-tuning cultivation management) |
| 3 | Feedback | Visualizing result data | Affective experience of intervention results (success/failure) |
| 4 | Articulation | Recording the link between experience and data | Logical connection of experience and ecosystem processes |
| 5 | Internalization | Fading (Gradual withdrawal) | Subconscious pattern recognition (Acquisition of intuition) |

*Note: Created by the author based on the SECI model (Nonaka & Takeuchi, 1995) and scaffolding theory (Pea, 2004; Reiser, 2004).*

### 4. Transforming Complexity from a "Threat" into a "Management Resource"
Let us consider what acquiring ecological intuition through scaffolding means for a farmer's management. For farmers in the early stages of transition, agricultural complexity is experienced as an "unmanageable risk." But for a farmer who has acquired ecological intuition, that same complexity begins to hold entirely different meanings. Weeds are not costs to be eliminated; depending on the species, they become resources for soil improvement and natural predator conservation. The uneven distribution of pests is not a threat; it becomes an indicator showing the health of the soil and vegetation. In-field heterogeneity is not a source of management costs; it becomes the rationale for deploying multiple crops and varieties in the right places.

What this transformation brings to farmers is managerial autonomy freed from dependence on external materials and external instructions. A state where one can make judgments while negotiating directly with the ecosystem of one's own field, without relying on the prescriptions of agrochemical and fertilizer manufacturers or AI system notifications—this is the state referred to by the "farmer autonomy" that agroecology aspires to.

Furthermore, this autonomy is the foundation of long-term resilience (managerial robustness). The "Law of Requisite Variety," formulated by cybernetics researcher W. Ross Ashby, states that "in order to control a system, the variety of the controlling system must be at least as great as the variety of the system to be controlled." Standardized systems increase efficiency by reducing internal variety, but they lose response options to unexpected changes. In contrast, an agricultural system that internalizes the inherent complexity of the land functions as a reservoir of diverse responses derived from that complexity itself. The robustness of agricultural management should be understood not as the "ability to avoid shocks," but as the "ability to maintain and adapt functions while learning from shocks." The scaffold will eventually be removed. But what remains within the farmer when the scaffold is taken away is the true achievement of technology.

**Table 3: Resilience Comparison of Agricultural Systems Based on the "Law of Requisite Variety"**

| Nature of System | Environmental Change (External Shock) | Response Process | Consequences for Resilience |
|---|---|---|---|
| Standardized System<br>(Elimination of complexity) | Expected (Known changes) | Uniform response based on manuals and prescriptions | Maintainable (Efficient processing) |
| | Unexpected (Unknown abnormal weather, etc.) | Lack of response options (Depletion of internal variety) | System collapse, exposure of vulnerability |
| Complexity Utilization System<br>(Agroecology) | Expected / Unexpected | Reading situations via ecological intuition and mobilizing specific resources | Adaptation and maintenance (Demonstration of buffering capacity) |

*Note: Created by the author based on Ashby's (1956) "Law of Requisite Variety".*

## Part 2: Technology Does Not Disappear, but Fades into the Background — The "Co-evolution" of Humans, Technology, and Ecosystems

### 1. Where Does Technology Go After the Scaffold is Removed?
Consider the act of removing a scaffold. When a steel pipe scaffold is dismantled at a construction site, the building already stands there. The scaffold vanishes; the building remains. The completion of Scaffolding depicted in Part 1 appears, at first glance, just like this—the AI system's instructions are no longer needed, and the farmer's ecological intuition remains. Technology disappears, capability remains.

But is this really true? After years of walking the field while gazing at sensor data, something is etched into the farmer's body. A vocabulary of observation: "When moisture is low, the angle of the leaves changes like this." A form of inquiry: "The question I should ask in this section during this season." The sensation of recognizing that the patterns shown by the sensors and one's own senses have begun to align. Technology has not "disappeared." It continues to live on in a different modality within the farmer's perception.

To answer this question, Part 2 introduces two theories. One is "Postphenomenology" by philosopher of technology Don Ihde—a framework that categorizes how humans form relations with technology. The other is "Actor-Network Theory (ANT)" by sociologists Bruno Latour and Michel Callon—a framework that describes agricultural practices as a network in which farmers, technology, and ecosystems are connected through chains of translation.

### 2. Describing Agricultural Practices as a Network
At the core of ANT is the "principle of generalized symmetry," which demands that we stop thinking only humans can drive situations. Methodologically, in analytical contexts, as long as soil microbes, climate, and sensors possess the capacity to substantially alter the behavior of other entities, they are treated as equivalent "actors."

When we look at agricultural practices from this perspective, the landscape changes. When a farmer reads sensor data and alters the timing of fertilization, the farmer is converting (translating) the sensor's measurement values into "their own judgment." The result of that intervention returns to the system as data, updating the system's model. The updated model generates new suggestions for the farmer, altering the farmer's subsequent behavior. This is the "circular chain of translation," and agricultural practice is understood as the dynamic product of this chain.

Here, a crucial question arises. The farmer's embodied practices—sniffing the soil, sensing subtle changes in leaf color, reading the soil resistance when stepped on—contain qualitative information that sensors cannot quantify. When the farmer intervenes based on this tacit knowledge, and the result feeds back into the system as data, the farmer's bodily knowledge is being "translated" into the system's explicit model. While Part 1 described a support flow where "the AI system scaffolds the farmer," viewed as a chain of translation, the farmer is also "teaching" the system. This bidirectionality supplements the full picture of agricultural practice that the vocabulary of Scaffolding could not fully capture.

### 3. Soil Microbes and Climate are "Active Mediators"
There are further participants in the chain of translation who are neither humans nor systems. Latour distinguishes actors into "mediators" and "intermediaries." While intermediaries merely pass on the influence of other actors without transforming it, mediators modify the very content of the influence as it passes through them, creating new differences. In the agricultural practice network, soil microbes, climate, and crops are "active mediators" in this sense.

In a field where the activity of specific rhizosphere microbes (arbuscular mycorrhizal fungi) has increased, the crop's phosphorus absorption autonomously improves, making supplementary fertilization interventions by the farmer unnecessary. When the farmer observes and records this "obviation of intervention," that record is translated into the system as a "no-fertilization-needed pattern," updating the model. The microbes change the farmer's judgment, changing the system through the farmer, and the altered system guides the farmer's next observation. Microbes are active participants that deform the chain of translation.

The process described in Part 1 as "the farmer altering their cognitive attitude toward complexity" becomes more precise when reread from this perspective. The shift from experiencing complexity as a "threat" to experiencing it as a "resource" is described not just as an internal shift in the farmer's attitude, but as a transformation of the translation relationship between the farmer and non-human actors. The stage experienced as a threat is a stage where translation relations are unformed or antagonistic. The stage where it can be experienced as a resource is understood as a stage where farmers and non-human actors have formed a "partial alliance"—a stage where they have mutually inscribed each other into the network.

### 4. Farmers' Embodied Knowledge Rewrites AI Systems — The SECI Model and the Bidirectionality of Translation
Let us organize the bidirectionality of translation by connecting it with the circular process of the SECI model (Nonaka & Takeuchi, 1995). Although the SECI model originally cycles in the order of S (Socialization) → E (Externalization) → C (Combination) → I (Internalization), when observed from the phase where tacit knowledge is externalized as data in the interaction between a farmer and an AI system, it corresponds as follows: The farmer's tacit knowledge is datafied in the form of observation records and intervention behaviors (Externalization), updating the model as input to the system (Combination). The updated system generates new suggestions for the farmer (Internalization), which are integrated into the farmer's next embodied practices (Socialization). Thus, the four modes of SECI complete a cycle, and this cycle is isomorphic to the cycle of ANT's translation chain.

When a farmer's input data is designed as "feedback that substantially contributes to updating the model"—when the farmer's agency is acknowledged by design—the system transforms into a structure that reflects the farmer's cognition. But the reverse is also true. When farmers form observation habits through long-term interaction with an AI system, the selection, weighting, and causal models of variables adopted in the system's design phase can act as an "invisible framework" for the farmer's ecological intuition. The farmer feels they are judging "autonomously," but in fact, they are perceiving along the epistemological premises of the system designer. This illustrates the hidden difficulty of "letting go of technology."

### 5. The Ontological Shift in Technological Relations — From "Tools" to "Transparent Infrastructure"
Philosopher Don Ihde categorized the relational modes that humans form with technology and the world into three types. In "Hermeneutic relations," technology generates a "text" of the world, and humans decipher it. This applies to the process where a farmer consciously interprets sensor numbers to estimate soil conditions; technology is in the foreground of the farmer's consciousness as a "sign to be read." In "Embodiment relations," technology becomes transparent as an extension of the body, and humans experience the world directly "through" the technology. This is akin to the sensation a farmer has when operating a handheld sensor, reading the field's conditions directly without being conscious of the operating procedures. In "Background relations," technology recedes out of the focal point of consciousness and functions as the prerequisite structure of world experience. This is the state where the observation habits the farmer formed through long-term interaction with the system are experienced as a "natural sense."

**Table 4: Ihde's Modes of Technological Relations and the Process of "Letting Go of Technology"**

| Stage | Relational Mode | Positioning of Technology | Farmer's Experience | Concrete Agricultural Example |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1. Dependence | Hermeneutic relation | In the foreground of consciousness | "Deciphering numbers to estimate soil condition" | Making fertilization decisions by looking at sensor values |
| 2. Transition | Embodiment relation | Transparent as an extension of the body | "Feeling of reading the field directly while operating the sensor" | Judging by oneself while using the device |
| 3. Letting Go | Background relation | Recedes out of consciousness | "Observation habits are rooted as a natural sense" | Knowing just by going to the field |

*Note: As the stages progress, there is a shift from "dependence" on technology to its "backgrounding." Stage 3 represents the state of having "let go of technology," corresponding to Heidegger's (1927) "readiness-to-hand (Zuhandenheit)." Created by the author based on Ihde's (1990) postphenomenology.*

The process described functionally in Part 1 as the "completion of fading" is redescribed through this framework as an ontological transformation of the relational mode between farmer and technology. Technology does not "withdraw" and disappear; it shifts its ontological status from an "explicit tool" to a "transparent cognitive infrastructure." This resonates deeply with the transition from "presence-at-hand (Vorhandenheit)" to "readiness-to-hand (Zuhandenheit)" described in Heidegger's tool analysis. When a skilled craftsman swings a hammer, the hammer is not experienced as an object of consciousness; it melts transparently into the act of driving the nail. The practical intuition of "using technology to let go of technology" acquires the language of philosophy of technology through this description. What is let go of is the "hermeneutic relation" with technology; technology becomes the implicit structural foundation for the farmer's engagement with the ecosystem by shifting into a "background relation."

### 6. The Dynamism of "Translational Co-evolution"
Synthesizing the discussion so far reveals the full picture of agricultural practice. In biology, co-evolution is the process in which multiple species adapt and transform over the long term while exerting selective pressures on each other. Part 2 formulates this as "Translational Co-evolution," a process involving the translational interactions of three parties: the farmer, the technological system, and the ecosystem.

In translational co-evolution, no single actor unilaterally transforms the others. The farmer learns from the system, changing the system; the system's transformation alters the farmer's cognitive tools; the farmer's altered practices change the ecosystem's responses; and those changes provoke the next adaptation in both the farmer and the system. This co-evolution has two layers operating on different time scales. In the short-to-medium-term layer, the relational mode between farmer and technology shifts (Hermeneutic → Embodiment → Background), forming a locally balanced state. In the long-term layer, technological updates, climate fluctuations, and changes in soil ecosystems temporarily disrupt that stability, and proficiency and re-stabilization stack up in a spiral manner.

**Table 5: The Interactive Structure of "Translational Co-evolution" Based on Actor-Network Theory**

| Direction of Interaction | Dynamics of "Translation/Mediation" in ANT | Concrete Examples in Agricultural Practice |
|---|---|---|
| Farmer ⇔ AI System | Externalization of data and model updating | Refinement of models through input of farmer's embodied knowledge (tacit knowledge) and the generation of new questions from AI |
| Farmer ⇔ Ecosystem | Cultivation intervention and ecological response | Changes in soil environment due to the farmer's meticulous management, and the corresponding growth of crops or fluctuations in pests |
| Ecosystem ⇔ AI System | Mediation by environmental factors and model obsolescence | Changes in data patterns due to microbial activity or abnormal weather (active mediators) and demands to reconstruct AI models |

*Note: Created by the author based on Actor-Network Theory by Latour (2005) and Callon (1986).*

In this spiral dynamic, the completion of Scaffolding—described in Part 1 as the "outcome of the learning process"—is repositioned from the perspective of translational co-evolution as the "new starting point of a much longer process." The moment the farmer lets go of their dependence on technology is also the moment the farmer becomes more deeply connected to the network of translational co-evolution. And the next question arises: Should this translational co-evolution be self-contained between one farmer and one piece of land? This is the inquiry of Part 3.

## Part 3: Opening Local Knowledge to the World — The Emergence of Digital Commons

### 1. The Dilemma of Locality — Can "Deeply Local Knowledge" be Shared?
The theoretical achievements built up in Parts 1 and 2, however, reveal a new quandary proportional to the depth of those achievements. Both parts have described the "deep adaptation" that occurs between a specific farmer and a specific piece of land. Over many years, the farmer establishes chains of translation with the microbiome, soil structure, and microclimate of that land, cultivating ecological intuition. Translational co-evolution is dense, and its value lies in that depth. However, that depth itself becomes the question: How can highly localized knowledge be shared?

Agroecological knowledge is inextricably tied to specific soil, microclimate, and ecosystem networks. The ecological intuition a farmer in Kyushu acquired through translational co-evolution cannot be directly applied to a farmer with different soil conditions in Tohoku. In agroecology, where complexity itself is the resource, simple copy-and-pasting of knowledge destroys the ecological context. This is the "Dilemma of Locality." The deeper one learns, the harder that knowledge is to transport. Part 3 responds to this question by connecting cognitive science's theory of "Distributed Cognition" with Elinor Ostrom's theory of the "Commons."

### 2. Circulating Not Answers, but "Forms of Inquiry"
The theory of "Distributed Cognition" by cognitive scientist Edwin Hutchins (1995) argues that human cognitive functions are not confined within an individual's brain but exist distributed across a network of interactions with others, tools, and the environment. In the navigation of large ships, the cognition of "where we should head" does not reside solely in the captain's head, but is achieved distributively through a chain of charts, compasses, and the physical movements and voices of the crew.

From this perspective, a crucial epistemological shift occurs. If knowledge is a "good inside the farmer's head," the problem of knowledge sharing becomes "how to extract and transport that good." But if knowledge is a "relationship within the chain of translation between farmer and ecosystem," it transforms into a design problem of mediation: "how to connect different sites of practice." It becomes clear here why the naive model of knowledge sharing—the "creation of universal manuals"—does not function in agroecology.

Then, what should be mediated? The concept of "Problematizing" established in Part 1 plays a central role here. The system should be designed not to "deliver answers," but to "deliver the forms of inquiry generated by the translational chains in other farmlands." This book calls this the "Circulation of Inquiry." "The question of how microclimate on a northern slope affects specific natural predator communities," generated within the translational co-evolution of Farmer A in Kyushu, is extracted not as specific data of Farm A, but as a "category of relationship to be observed," and is delivered as a "prompt" to Farmer B in another region with similar topographical variations. Farmer B does not receive this as an "answer," but uses it as a starting point for new observation within the unique context of their own land.

However, one reservation is necessary. To borrow philosopher Polanyi's words, "we can know more than we can tell"—the formalizable part of tacit knowledge is only a fraction. The smell of the soil, the resistance when stepped on—these are physical sensations that cannot even be reduced to "forms of inquiry," and they are still acquired exclusively through the translational co-evolution driven by each farmer's embodied practice. The "Circulation of Inquiry" is not a substitute for this embodied practice, but merely a mediation for its activation and orientation. Yet, its role of "merely being a mediation" is the key to scaling out.

### 3. Conditions for AI Systems as Boundary Objects
What design principles must AI systems have to mediate the "Circulation of Inquiry"? The concept of "Boundary Objects," proposed by Star & Griesemer (1989), becomes effective here. A boundary object is an artifact that bridges the boundaries when communities with different expertise or contexts collaborate. While it is used specifically in each local setting, it possesses the flexibility to maintain a common structure overall.

The key to a digital agricultural system functioning as a boundary object lies in designing the system in two layers: a "local model (the product of translational co-evolution with each farmer)" and a "common query interface (the structure that forms and circulates inquiries)." Farmer A's system and Farmer B's system have different local models but share a common protocol that mediates the "Circulation of Inquiry." The design of this protocol is the substance of the AI system as a boundary object. In her later years, Star warned that immense "infrastructural work" is required for boundary objects to function. This warning connects to the theory of commons in the next section.

### 4. The Creation of Asynchronous, Non-local "Weak Ties"
What kind of community emerges when the "Circulation of Inquiry" functions between farmers? Sociologist Mark Granovetter's (1973) theory of "The Strength of Weak Ties" demonstrated that weak ties—sparse relationships spanning different communities—function more effectively in disseminating new information and diverse perspectives than strong ties. The "Circulation of Inquiry" among geographically and culturally distant farmers structurally creates exactly these "weak ties."

Farmers do not need to know the details of each other's farmlands. A loose communality as "practitioners pursuing the same category of inquiry" is sufficient. This "community of practice built on weak ties" is characterized not by common farming methods, but as a network sharing a common observational language and a culture of inquiry. However, there are conditions for participation. Those who can meaningfully participate in the "Circulation of Inquiry" are farmers who have reached at least a minimum translational context—a stage where they are establishing an embodiment relation with technology—on their own land. For farmers in the early stages of transition, the structures of Scaffolding and translational co-evolution described in Parts 1 and 2 remain effective.

### 5. Designing a Digital Agroecology Commons
When considering institutional design to sustain the "Circulation of Inquiry" as a network, economist Elinor Ostrom's theory of the "Commons" provides a powerful framework. Garrett Hardin's (1968) theory of "The Tragedy of the Commons" argued that shared resources inevitably deplete due to the pursuit of individual rationality. Ostrom (1990) empirically criticized this, showing that commons exist worldwide where stakeholders autonomously design and operate rules to sustainably manage resources over the long term without relying on the state or the market, and she extracted their "design principles."

Farmers' tacit knowledge, ecological intuition, and the products of translational co-evolution constitute a knowledge commons that does not deplete through sharing. However, without appropriate institutional design, "another form of tragedy" arises—exploitation through platform capitalism via the enclosure of knowledge, or the collapse of collaboration due to a lack of mutual trust among farmers.

Three of Ostrom's design principles are particularly relevant. First, "clearly defined boundaries"—the distinction must be institutionally guaranteed that forms of inquiry are subject to sharing, while the raw data of a farmer's land belongs to their privacy. Second, "collective-choice arrangements"—farmers must be able to participate in changing the model design, variable selection, and weighting of the AI system. Third, "nested enterprises"—a nested structure where local farmer groups hold independent rule-design authority while participating in broader networks simultaneously ensures both the diversity and resilience of the commons.

When this nested structure functions, the resilience of a single farmland and the network resilience of the commons function synergistically. When an "unknown shock" occurs in a certain region due to climate change, observational categories mediated through the "Circulation of Inquiry" by farmers in other regions may trigger new questions. However, a caveat is also needed. The circulation of inquiry itself harbors the risk of generating another form of rigidity—"standardization of inquiry," where each farmer converges on the dominant form of inquiry on the network. If the "collective-choice arrangements" fail to function, the commons can become an apparatus that consumes diversity rather than generating it.

Across the three parts, the question of "how to let go of technology" has acquired new dimensions. Part 1 provided the answer of "farmer autonomy," and Part 2 added ontological precision that "letting go means technology transitioning into 'readiness-to-hand'." ## Conclusion: The Double "Letting Go" and the Future Beyond

### 1. The First Letting Go — Letting Go of Dependence on Dictating Machines
What this book has consistently questioned is the state of the relationship between technology and farmers. What smart agriculture has provided to farmers are primarily "answers." Optimal fertilizer amounts, optimal harvest timings, optimal pesticide selections—the system provides the answers, and the farmer follows them. This relationship appears efficient, but it continuously reproduces a structure that makes farmers dependent on the system. If the answer-providing system stops, the farmer is left at a loss.

"Technology as a scaffold," discussed in Part 1, fundamentally transforms the design of this relationship. What technology should provide to farmers is not answers, but questions. "Why is this section slow to dry?" "What does the increase of this weed signify?" By posing questions, the farmer begins the practice of "reading" their own field. Over a long period, "ecological intuition" is nurtured within the farmer. When this intuition grows, technology traces the process described in Part 2. From "hermeneutic relations" to "embodiment relations," and eventually to "background relations." "Letting go of technology" does not mean discarding the technology. It means letting go of the "dependent relational mode" with the technology, taming the technology to serve as the transparent premise of one's own perception. This is the First Letting Go.

### 2. The Second Letting Go — Letting Go of Closed Autonomy and Connecting to a Network
However, this book does not end with the First Letting Go. What should the autonomous farmer let go of next? The deep knowledge a farmer acquires through translational co-evolution is unique to that specific land. That depth is a value, but at the same time, it harbors a rigidity: closed autonomy, or the "monopolization of knowledge confined only to one's own land."

The Second Letting Go is to release this closed autonomy. When autonomous farmers participate in the "Circulation of Inquiry," they hand over the forms of inquiry generated by their own translational co-evolution to the network. That inquiry might trigger observation for another farmer with different soil and a different climate. Conversely, an inquiry one does not yet possess might arrive from the translational co-evolution of a distant farmland. This is not the abandonment of the farmer's autonomy. While deepening the translational co-evolution with the ecosystem of their own land, the farmer contributes to the adaptive capacity of a broader network through the "Circulation of Inquiry," and receives benefits from it in return. Being local and connecting globally are not contradictory.

### 3. Unresolved Questions Toward Empirical Verification and Social Implementation
The logical chain of the three parts presented in this book—Scaffolding, Translational Co-evolution, and Digital Agroecology Commons—have all been proposed as "theoretical hypotheses awaiting empirical verification." There are quite a few unresolved questions. Does the farmer's tacit knowledge truly improve the system through translational inscription? Can the "shift to background relations" of technology actually be confirmed in farmers' long-term observations? Is extracting "forms of inquiry" technically feasible? And can commons-based governance be established under the realistic conditions of a declining farming population and digital literacy gaps?

These are not weaknesses of the theory, but a research agenda opened up by the theory. The value of the vision outlined in this book will only be evaluated by inducing empirical research on these questions and inspiring trials on the ground in agricultural practices. There is one thing I can say with certainty: when farmers begin to experience the complexity of their fields not as a "threat" but as a "resource," the landscape of agriculture will change. That change is the substance of the transition to agroecology, and the ultimate foundation of all the theoretical constructs discussed in this book.

### 4. Final Thoughts: Theory as a Foundation for Muddy Practices
While writing this book, a sensation repeatedly returned to me. The more I piled up theories, the further I felt I was drifting from the muddy reality of the farmlands. Vygotsky's ZPD, Ihde's postphenomenology, Latour's ANT, Ostrom's theory of commons—when these words stack up, I find myself questioning whether they truly illuminate the reality of agriculture.

But simultaneously, I have a real sense that things invisible without theory became visible through theory. The confusion of "what is this data?" when I first started using sensors. The sensation of the moment I realized years later, "I know without looking at the sensors, just by going to the field." When I was able to articulate that this was not mere "habituation" but an ontological shift from hermeneutic relations to background relations, I became able to see a little further across my own practice.

Theory does not replace practice. It illuminates practice, is illuminated by practice, and together they are updated. The cycle of "using technology to let go of technology" described in this book spins its wheels in the void if separated from the concrete foundation of the field. However, words that allow us to see far without losing that foundation are also necessary. As an attempt to offer theory as a foundation for muddy practices, I lay down my pen here.

## Afterword: On the Creation Process of This Manuscript
This book was written centering on the author's own agricultural practices and theoretical inspirations, utilizing LLMs (Claude Sonnet 4.6 and Gemini 3.1 Pro) as cognitive partners. Specifically, I received AI assistance in organizing existing theories across multiple disciplines, interactively structuring the logic, drafting each chapter, and translating this document.

This writing process itself is not unrelated to the theme of this book. "Technology as a cognitive partner," discussed in Part 1, was not just about agriculture for the author. As I proceeded with writing the manuscript, AI functioned not as a "machine that gives answers," but as a "partner to reconstruct questions with." When the author roughly threw out ideas saying "I want to say something like this," the AI returned it structured in theoretical vocabulary. Looking at that structure, the author questioned back, "No, that's not quite it." In this back-and-forth, inspirations that the author could not initially articulate slowly took shape. This might have been Scaffolding. Or perhaps a form of translational co-evolution.

However, I want to clarify one point. The core question of this book—"How to use technology to let go of technology"—was born from the author's own agricultural practices, not from dialogues with AI. Five years of trial and error in the field, nights staring at sensor data, the moment I noticed one morning "I understand without looking at the data anymore"—the foundation of the theory lies there. AI built a scaffold on top of that foundation. The scaffold is removed with the completion of this book.

I leave this afterword as a record of practice of the "cognitive partnership" depicted in this book.
